# Grease Trap vs Grease Interceptor

Grease traps and grease interceptors are plumbing devices designed to capture fats, oils, and grease (FOG) from wastewater before it enters sewer systems.

Although both devices perform the same function, they differ in size, location, and capacity.

---

## What Is a Grease Trap?

A grease trap is a smaller device that captures grease from wastewater produced in kitchens.

Grease traps are typically:

- Installed indoors
- Located under sinks or inside kitchens
- Used in smaller food service operations

As wastewater flows through the trap, grease floats to the surface and solids settle at the bottom. The separated wastewater then exits the device.

---

## What Is a Grease Interceptor?

A grease interceptor is a larger grease separation device designed for high-volume kitchens and food service operations.

Grease interceptors are typically:

- Installed outside the building
- Located underground
- Designed to handle larger wastewater volumes

Because of their larger capacity, grease interceptors are often used by restaurants and commercial kitchens with heavy grease production.

---

## Key Differences Between Grease Traps and Grease Interceptors

| Feature | Grease Trap | Grease Interceptor |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| Size | Smaller capacity | Larger capacity |
| Installation location | Indoors | Outdoors or underground |
| Typical users | Small kitchens or food prep areas | Restaurants and large commercial kitchens |
| Maintenance | Requires regular cleaning | Requires periodic pumping |

---

## Why Both Devices Require Maintenance

Both grease traps and grease interceptors accumulate grease and food waste over time.

Without regular cleaning, buildup can cause:

- Plumbing blockages
- Sewer line problems
- Kitchen odors
- Sanitation concerns

Routine maintenance removes accumulated grease and helps the system function properly.

---

## Which One Does a Restaurant Need?

The type of grease management system required often depends on:

- Restaurant size
- Cooking volume
- Local plumbing codes
- Municipal regulations

Some restaurants use indoor grease traps, while larger facilities may require an outdoor grease interceptor.
